INSPIRED BY A TRUE STORY/PAPER



INTRODUCTION



This project is going to focus on finding a good research paper that was presented at CHI in recent years, delve into a question/ problem that the paper brought up and ways to solve it by creating a prototype using Figma. The paper I chose was 12 Ways to Empower: Designing for Children’s Digital Autonomy. This dives into ways by which children can learn and be autonomous when using gadgets. The paper highlighted some design mechanisms that have proven to help children in this regard, and I leveraged some of them in the creation of my tool.

PROCESS OF PICKING AN AREA



I ended up looking at multiple papers by scrolling through both CHI 2024 and CHI 2023 contents for about 2 hours just to find a good paper that I was interested in. What I was really interested in had to do with blind people being able to learn how to read but required a physical prototype which was not possible in my case, so I landed on two others that seemed interesting. My top 2 picks were 12 Ways to Empower: Designing for Children’s Digital Autonomy and Challenges and Opportunities for Interactive Technology to Support Parents of HIV-Positive Children in Ethiopia in the Disclosure Process. Soon after, reading both abstracts and skimming through the contents, I decided on 12 Ways to Empower: Designing for Children’s Digital Autonomy.







ANSWERS TO SOME QUESTIONS BASED ON THE DESCRIPTION OF THE ORIGINAL PAPER



Below, I will be answering some questions based on the description of the original paper to help me deduce the important areas to focus on and how to think about creating my prototype.

1. What was the central problem or question of the paper? (This should be the same problem/question that your project is inspired by!)
The central questions of the paper were:
RQ1: How does the HCI literature conceptualize digital autonomy for children?

RQ2: What autonomy-supportive design mechanisms have been explored in apps and systems for children?

2. What did the authors do to answer this question or solve this problem?
To answer RQ1, the authors applied a qualitative analysis process through an open-coding approach by first looking for any existing conceptualization on the term “autonomy” and If the paper did not specifically mention the term autonomy, they read through the whole paper to identify what goals or purpose they were trying to support in relation to helping children navigate the digital environment. They then consolidated these into three groups of conceptualizations around digital autonomy.

To answer RQ2, the authors aimed to identify the design mechanisms used for supporting children’s digital autonomy, so they applied a qualitative analysis process once more through an open-coding approach. They paid extra attention to descriptions around the incentives, motivation, theoretical and ethnographic groundings behind the design by noting down the design details through which it tried to support autonomy (e.g., use of gaming elements, introducing peer collaboration elements, setting default goals for children). They then grouped the prototypes with similar designs which were then compared and consolidated, based on the ways in which children’s digital autonomy is meant to be supported (e.g., through providing external information input, through providing social context). This process led to a total of five high-level categories.

3. If there is a tool presented in the paper, did the authors present design requirements for it? If so, what are they?
No, there was no tool presented in the paper, but they did discuss some autonomy design mechanisms that can help children make informed decisions and to have digital autonomy.
They were:
• Scaffolding
Providing the appropriate assistance such external information inputs that will give the student enough of a “boost” to achieve the task .
Types

 Just In Time Prompt
 Informative interaction
 Scaffold of your own

• Decomposing
Provides a natural way of formalizing tacit knowledge through breaking down and providing analogs — “a familiar scenario to think with” , which has a critical place to help children explicitly link their existing knowledge to the more abstract digital concepts.
Types
 Storytelling
 Gamification

• Peer Support
o Encourage social interaction between children and their peers in order to promote their digital autonomy.
Types
 Peer Collaboration
 Peer Comparison

• Digital playground o These designs encourage children to freely interact with a digital system which is connected with a physical artefact, thus extending the playground to the physical environment around them.

• Nudging o These were designed to impose subtle design changes that could alter children’s behaviors and reinforce positive ones and is closely related to the dual process theories for a graphical representation and summary.









ANSWERS TO SOME QUESTIONS BASED ON THE DESCRIPTION OF WHAT I AM GOING TO DO



Your description of what you’re going to do must include answers to the following: 1. What are you going to make?

I am planning on making a prototype for an application that aims to use some of the autonomy design mechanisms to help children make digitally autonomous decisions.

2. For your project to satisfyingly solve the problem posed, what does it have to do? (e.g., What information does it have to display? Who does it have to work for? etc) Be specific!

To solve the questions posed, I will make sure that three autonomy design mechanisms are included in the prototype. The designs I have decided to implement are :
o Scaffolding - specifically just-in-time prompts
o Decomposing - specifically storytelling
o Nudging - specifically fear alert

Who does it have to work for? I will also make sure it is intended for children, so making the interface easy to navigate and understand. Since it is for kids, it has to be child protected- meaning there will always be measures in place to make sure that the contents displayed will only be kid friendly. There will be no unnecessary popups but only those to give encouragements and positive alerts.

I will specifically aim for children who are between 0-5 years because they learn very fast and as has already been discussed in the paper, children between those ages are exposed to gadgets really early so what they learn on it is what they will remember for future use.

What information does it have to display? It will be an educational app leveraging different methods to make learning fun so they will be learning objects, watching videos to learn basic math, watch some stories that will teach them about daily life, about strangers and online privacy. It will display information that helps children learn at different stages. So, starting off easy which does not require any prior formal knowledge and allows immediate exploration and soon after, some flexibility for different ages.

3. How is what you are going to make different from what is already in the paper?

The paper did not highlight a tool being built and did not talk about all/most of the autonomy design mechanisms in one prototype. I will try to have most of these components in one prototype that I will make.

4. If your paper does not include design requirements, write 3 to 5 of your own to follow. If it does, you can use those to inform your design!

For the paper, there were some design requirements for future works: o Identifying critical points of intervention can be of paramount importance. To start with, we recommend designers to consider identifying the critical points that could maximize influence.

o Proposing that future designs should put more emphasis on promoting self-generated knowledge in children — enable children to think critically on issues instead of just habituate to the instructions/cues or being nudged without even realizing it.

o Considering translating short-term boosts into longitudinal benefits.

o Considering children’s diverse needs and backgrounds — differentiation and personalization is critical to the effectiveness of designs.

o Ensuring the prototype allows a low floor, high ceiling for children. Here, “low floor” refers to an easy start that does not require any prior formal knowledge and allows immediate exploration; and a “high ceiling” enables more flexibility and provides room for children to grow and explore, especially for those children who were older or with more advanced ability.

Adding some more pages for functionality would be a plus as well for future works. The strengths that the prototype has are that there is great functionality displayed which highlights some key points raised above and it is not mentally overwhelming to interact with. Overall, the prototype needs a few touch ups to be perfect.
