A Design Manifesto: What I took out of HCI




Human-Computer Interaction is a multidisciplinary field that studies the interaction between humans and computers, with emphasis on designing technology that enhances the user experience.

As someone profoundly zealous about both design and technology, I was elated to take this class. My initial thought when I decided to take this class was that I was just going to see how computers and humans interacted. Little did I know, It was going to be much more specified to incorporate designing technology for an audience with the main goal of improving user experience.


Over the course of the semester, I engaged in five different projects that helped me to dive into the different questions involving Human-Computer Interaction.
They are:
How do we figure out what real people need?
How do we design tools that fit needs (and don’t suck)?
How do we know if we did a good job?
How did we get here (& where are we going)?

Given that I spent a whole semester learning about Human-Computer Interaction, I will give an overview of how my perspective of HCI changed throughout the semester and share my key takeaways from this course.









My key takeaways



Defining goals and setting ideation in



The first thing I learned in this class was to appreciate the double diamond design process. It is important to clearly define the goal you are trying to achieve and the audience you are aiming for. I relied heavily on the first two stages of the double diamond design process for the first two weeks of class as we spent time talking about how to figure out what people need . This delved into the introduction of creating a persona with a goal and a set of challenges preventing them from achieving that goal.



For my first project - Designing for Other People, I was tasked with coming up with an idea for an app that could help overcome a challenge that the people in my life were facing. I identified some goals but because I spoke to different people, the number of goals and frustrations increased. I fulfilled the discover aspect of the double diamond. It was really easy for me to set an audience for my designs but relatively harder to narrow down the set of goals I had gathered to define just one for which the theme of the technology designed will be centered around. I narrowed it down to 2 goals and 5 challenges.



Now, I had to set in motion the ideas I had in mind to find the best design. This dealt heavily with the ideation stage of HCI design process. I will say right now that sketches are the way to go when you start brainstorming about an idea you want to see come to life. It is easier to sketch and your ideas and see them in front of you before going to implement it later. I say this because if you dive into implementing the design straight away, you are bound to waste more time trying to figure out what to do when you could have sketched them out then decide on a design and then implement. This fulfilled the design aspect of the double diamond.

Looking at these sketches from Designing for Other People, I was able to quickly decide which aspects of my sketches I loved most and from there talk extensively about it. From here, I can easily open Figma and bring all my ideas into one in no time.





Right Questions & Feedback are the heart of HCI



How you ask questions determine the kind of feedback you receive and there are no two ways about it. This answers the question “How do we know if we did a good job?”. Throughout all my projects, one thing I have constantly had to do was to ask good questions in order to get great responses to carry out a design or great feedback to improve upon a design. I know I have to ask people what they want to see but how do I ask in a way so as to collect detailed explanations? This is where surveys and interviews come into play. It is super important that once you decide on a design, developed it and have made it available for users to interact with it, you ask good questions to try and get their feelings and reactions to the product. Users usually catch on to the little things and that will help you to improve on the product as well. For my fourth project - Seeking & Integrating Feedback, I redesigned a website I had previously designed based off of feedback I got from users through the use of surveys. It was really important that I asked the right questions.



There are some questions you must avoid in your surveys if you want in-depth feedback

1. Avoid “multi barreled questions
To get feedback for a specified aspect of prototype or design, you must make sure your questions are clear especially when the person/people you are asking are not with you to ask clarifying questions. It is always good to ask, “When do you eat breakfast?” than “when do you eat breakfast and dinner?”. An answer to the second question may be 3pm and you would not know which of the parts of the question It is answering and hence this feedback does not mean much.

2. You should try to avoid leading questions
You should never assume anything for anyone. Leading questions can sometimes make the user want to respond with answers that they think the designer will prefer to see than what they actually want to say. If I ask, “was our service helpful today?”, it implies that you are assuming the service was helpful when in actual fact it could have been very unhelpful.

3. Explanation is key
It is always good to explain or define terms if you are aware, it may have different meanings for different people and always keep questions relatively short.





Cherishing The Iterative Nature of Design



There is no end to design but rather always room for improvement. Our world is rapidly changing and so must our designs. Design is never a done deal as it is always evolving. Once we create a design and send it off for feedback, we must act on the given feedback to improve the design to better cater for the specified audience the design was meant for.



For my fourth project - Seeking & Integrating Feedback as mentioned before, I redesigned a prototype I had previously designed and for this, I had to make sure my prototype was in good shape to be sent out to the target audience to test it out and give me some feedback to improve on the project. This is where I asked the survey questions and once I got the responses, went back to my design and prototype to make some changes. The feedback was the reason I was able to go back and update my designs according to the needs of the users because if I am not trying to maximize user experience, what is the point of even asking for feedback. Because I was able to ask for feedback, I changed my designed to make them better. I did not say “even though I have gotten feedback, I will stick with my design as it seems great to me”. I can design today and come up with a better design tomorrow or 10 days from now. In short, design is not STATIC.

Meeting the user’s needs



Human-Computer interaction is all about boosting user experience and meeting the needs of the users by designing technology that will impact the users in a positive way. So, going back to the question “How do we know if we did a good job?” . To answer this question, we will talk about cognitive walkthroughs and heuristic evaluations.
A cognitive walkthrough involves defining a task, letting the designer step through the task, and then allowing the user to step through the task as well. These walkthroughs access the learnability of a system. Let’s take Instagram for example, if my friend has never used it before and she tries to navigate the app by following a specified task, how do you think she will do? Will she struggle to find some buttons and pages continuously or will she eventually learn and will it be easy or hard for her. If it turns out to be hard, then the system must be reevaluated to allow for some improvements because the goal of a cognitive walkthrough is always to make sure the system is easily accessible by an audience.



This is a cognitive walkthrough I did for Seeking & Integrating Feedback. From the image above, we can see that I stepped through the defined task with the defined set of questions. These are the questions that are always going to be asked for a cognitive walkthrough no matter the system



Now for the heuristic evaluation. It is a metric which helps in identifying design problems in a user interface. It helps evaluators judge the design/ system against a set of guidelines called heuristics. Read more about them here.
I was mostly using Jakob Nielsen’s 10 usability heuristics. If the system is usable, how are you determining it? What set of heuristics are you evaluating it against? Once you have answered these questions and your system / design checks out then it is usable.

Now, to the answer the question. If the system is usable and assessing learnability is easy, then we know we did a good job in designing the system as it will meet the needs of the user. But remember, design is not static so the set of heuristics you evaluate your system against may change which will require the design of the system to be modified.



Utilize visual hierarchy



Understanding visual hierarchy is necessary if you want to have an efficient design. Gestalt’s principles can really help when designing for an audience. As we know, many people cannot pay attention to too many things all at once. It overwhelms the users as they would not know what to look at first so incorporating gestalt’s principles into your iterative design is a must. When trying to relay information to others, I know that some information holds more importance than others so placing that information first will draw users attention to it.





In that, what feelings am I trying to get out of the audience? What should be engraved in their minds? With the help of colors, sizes, placement, and movement, it can happen.










How did I get here (& where am I going)?




I love designing and for all my projects, I was tasked to design a tool/prototype in Figma. From the inspiration of an idea that was captured in the form of sketches to implementing a finished prototype was worth it. Using the 7 principles of design as my backbone, I prioritized every single project and poured out my best for it and they paid off in the end.

Amid the joyous achievements, a topic that was most challenging was reading HCI research papers. In my honest opinion, finding a good research paper that catches the eye to make you want to read the paper thoroughly is truly a hassle especially for a beginner. I could read multiple abstracts and find them interesting but once I start to skim through the contents, I quickly lose interest.

For my fifth project - Inspired by a True Story/Paper, I was tasked to find a paper and design a prototype in Figma that was inspired by the design requirements or problem statement described in the paper I chose. I persevered, read through the paper and all I can say is that it was worth it in the end because now I can say I can read a research paper and dissect it to find the important information I need at a given time. This will also help me with future endeavors involving research as I know the basics to build upon it.

A valuable piece of HCI for me would be to redesign for my audience after collecting their feedback. Was it easy to learn? Was it hard? It is not always about the aesthetics of a design but also how people can easily navigate the system with little to no problem.

By employing human-centered design principles, prioritizing feedback while incorporating design iteration and understanding visual hierarchy, I am more aware of the powerful tools I possess to make even better systems in the near future to meet every user’s needs.
